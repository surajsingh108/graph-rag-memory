Albert Einstein was born on 14 March 1879 in Ulm, a city in the Kingdom of Württemberg within the German Empire. His father, Hermann Einstein, was a salesman and engineer, and his mother was Pauline Koch. The family relocated to Munich shortly after Albert's birth, where his father and uncle founded a company manufacturing electrical equipment based on direct current.

As a child, Einstein showed a deep curiosity about the natural world. A compass given to him at age five fascinated him: the needle's invisible guidance convinced him that empty space concealed forces no one could see. He was considered a slow talker as a toddler, but by his school years his teachers recognised an unusually strong capacity for independent thought.

Einstein attended the Luitpold Gymnasium in Munich. At fifteen, after his family moved to Pavia in Italy, he followed later and completed his secondary schooling in Aarau, Switzerland. In 1896 he enrolled at the Swiss Federal Polytechnic School in Zürich, training as a teacher of physics and mathematics, and graduated in 1900.

Unable to find an academic post after graduation, Einstein took a job in 1902 as a technical expert (third class) at the Swiss Patent Office in Bern. The position left him time to think, and in 1905 he published four landmark papers. That year is known as his annus mirabilis — his miracle year. The papers covered Brownian motion, the photoelectric effect, special relativity, and the equivalence of mass and energy, summarised in the equation E = mc².

The special theory of relativity, published in June 1905, showed that the speed of light in a vacuum is constant for all observers regardless of their motion, and that space and time are not fixed but relative to the observer. A decade later, in November 1915, Einstein presented the general theory of relativity to the Prussian Academy of Sciences. General relativity recast gravity not as a force but as the curvature of spacetime produced by mass and energy. It predicted that light rays passing near a massive object would bend — a prediction confirmed during the solar eclipse of 29 May 1919 by astronomer Arthur Eddington, making Einstein internationally famous overnight.

In 1921 Einstein was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for his explanation of the photoelectric effect, which demonstrated that light consists of discrete quanta (later called photons). The Nobel Committee considered general relativity too speculative at the time.

When the Nazi Party rose to power in Germany in January 1933, Einstein — who was Jewish and on an academic visit to the United States — decided not to return. He took up a position at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey, where he would remain for the rest of his life. He became an American citizen in 1940.

In August 1939 Einstein co-signed a letter to President Franklin D. Roosevelt warning that Germany might be pursuing nuclear weapons derived from uranium fission. The letter helped prompt the establishment of the Manhattan Project, though Einstein himself was not invited to participate because military authorities considered him a security risk.

Einstein spent his later years searching, without success, for a unified field theory that would combine electromagnetism and gravity into a single framework. He died on 18 April 1955 in Princeton, New Jersey, of a ruptured aortic aneurysm, at the age of seventy-six. He had refused surgery, saying he did not want to prolong his life artificially.

Albert Einstein is widely regarded as one of the greatest physicists in history. His special and general theories of relativity transformed humanity's understanding of space, time, and gravity, and his name has become a byword for genius.
