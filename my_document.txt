The philosopher Thales of Miletus, a student of Pythagoras, is often credited with being the first to think about ‘everything is water.’ But the idea wasn’t purely intuitive. Thales, a merchant by trade, observed the transformative power of water – its ability to erode stone, dissolve salt, and sustain life. He reasoned that if water could accomplish so much, it must be the fundamental substance of the universe. 

However, Thales’ belief wasn't without criticism. His nephew, Anaximenes, argued that air was the true primordial element. Anaximenes suggested that air, through condensation and rarefaction, could transform into other substances – water, earth, and fire. He proposed a cyclical process: dense air condensed to form water, which then solidified to become earth, and finally, heated to produce fire.

The debate between Thales and Anaximenes represents a crucial turning point in Western thought – the transition from mythological explanations of the world to rational, observable reasoning. It highlights the importance of empirical evidence and logical deduction in understanding the universe.  Furthermore, it raises a fundamental question: are we observing reality, or are our perceptions shaping our understanding?
